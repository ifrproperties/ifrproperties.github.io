Short sales an Alternative to Foreclosure

Posted on February 25, 2015 by Glenn Antoine in Real Estate Investing


Being a homeowner is something a lot of people dreams of and anyone who gets to purchase a house is something to be proud of. Financial problems, however, can put a damper to your goals. The good news is if you can no longer afford to pay your mortgage, one of two things can happen. Either your home would be foreclosed or you can dispose it through a short sale. The latter is often considered the lesser of two evils, what with the options it can provide a distressed homeowner.

What is it exactly?

A short sale is where you sell your home for less than the remaining balance on your mortgage. Although it does not pay off all your debts, it mitigates additional fees and costs that a creditor will continue to incur until the entire loan amount has been settled. It is often executed to prevent a home foreclosure. While it is considered an alternative, the possibility of a short sale still lies on whether or not a lender approves a proposed sale. Most lenders have pre-determined criteria for this type of estate transaction.  Nonetheless, it is a solution that both the creditor and lender could benefit from.

A short sale is not for everyone, however. It is only applicable under certain circumstances:

  • You are not qualified for a mortgage refinancing or modification
  • You are unable to make mortgage payments or you owe more than your home’s current worth
  • You are facing long-term financial hardship
  • You failed to sell your home at the price that will pay off your mortgage with some interest or profit

You are more than ready to leave your home

Understanding the Sale Process

A short sale is similar to other real estate transactions, where real estate agents are still involved, except for differences in certain points. It is often initiated by you, the homeowner, and not the lender, which is why there is a risk of a short sale not pulling through. The sale is also done through a realtor and not auctioned at a Trustee Sale that usually happens with foreclosed properties.

The sale starts with you setting a sale price that should be based on current market value. If the market is performing well, you have a higher chance of negotiating a sale price that could pay off the entire mortgage debt.

The rest of the procedure is as follows:

  • Propose a sale
    Once you have collected all pertinent financial information, you will then have to negotiate with other lien holders, if applicable. You can only hope and pray that they all agree on the offer you propose.
  • Review acceptable offers
    To generate leads on potential buyers, marketing should be a priority. When working with a reputable real estate agent, you might get a better deal based on their expertise and efforts alone. Once the offer starts coming in, the next thing you need to do is review them and choose those that are acceptable.
  • Agree to the terms of the sale
    Have you already found a buyer that meets your needs for a viable short sale? You will then have to agree to the terms, and ensure that the deal will not fall through and put you in deeper debt.

    To finalize the deal, you must work with a mortgage lender and the real estate agent of the buyer.

    Say the lender agrees to a short sale because it offers a smaller financial loss as opposed to foreclosure. You will be able to, for the lack of a better word, enjoy several benefits.

Reduce mortgage debt

Once the lender agrees to remove or release the lien on a property, you would then have the authority to sell it at a price that could pay off all your debt or reduce the remaining balance. In most cases, the mortgage amount is only reduced rather than paid in full. It is then your responsibility to repay the remaining balance. Between settling a significant amount and just a few thousands, a short sale is definitely a better alternative.

There are also cases when you might receive relocation assistance that you can use for moving expenses.

Lessen credit implications

Both short sale and foreclosure have an adverse effect on your credit score, but the latter is believed to have a severe negative impact. Typically your score will drop between 200 and 400 point, and will remain on your report for 7 years. myFICO begs to differ, however, saying that short sales, foreclosures, and deeds in lieu of foreclosures are all considered not paid as agreed accounts for purposes of your FICO score.

Sounds damaging, doesn’t it? With a short sale, however, you have higher chances of repairing your credit score sooner rather than later.

Immediate purchase on your next home

Failure to pay your mortgage loan often results in restrictions imposed on future home purchases. If you had your home foreclosed, you can only buy another property after 5 years with restrictions or after 7 years with no restrictions. But following a short sale, you can purchase another home immediately, provided that you have the funds to buy one, of course. With likelihood to receive relocation assistance, the transition from your old home to the new one is also easier.

Better chances at getting a future loan

A foreclosure must be reported on future loan applications, which might give lenders a reason to disapprove your application. Although not a guarantee, record of the short sale you initiated may or may not be reported in your future loan application. This gives you higher chances of being pre-approved. If this is not the case, you can always take steps to getting a mortgage again. You can obtain a letter from the lender that will be used as proof that the previous loan was settled in a short sale. You should also order a copy of your credit report from three major national credit bureaus — Equifax, Experian and TransUnion. You will then use all this paperwork to get pre-approved.

This real estate transaction, however, is clearly not without disadvantages. Apart from scars on your credit report, it also has tax implications since a successful short sale reduces debt and is considered an income by the IRS. Red tape is also prevalent in this type of process, similar to what will happen with REO and HUD properties, where multiple levels of approvals and conditions are required.

With junior liens or mechanic’s lien holders involved, there is a huge possibility that they can prevent a sale. This reduces your chances of getting out of financial trouble that might have been resolved through a payoff.
