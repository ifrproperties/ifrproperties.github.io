Understanding the Foreclosure Process in Indiana

Having your home foreclosed can be likened to a judge dropping the gavel to mark the finality of his decision — irrevocable and with no turning back. This could also mean that you will be homeless in a matter of days. But understanding the foreclosure process in Indiana would make the entire event less damaging on your part.

Foreclosure is a legal process where a lender takes a property to pay off a loan. This is after a borrower stops paying his mortgage. It is a really stressful event, especially for the borrower, as he is not only piling up debts, but is also at risk of losing his home.

How foreclosure is handled different from one state to the next. If you live in Indiana, don’t hesitate to give us a call. We can help you in understanding the foreclosure rules and procedures.

Judicial Foreclosure

In Indiana, the most common foreclosure procedure is judicial, where a foreclosing party or mortgage lender files a lawsuit in a bid to foreclose a home. You, the borrower, will then receive a letter from the court demanding that you make payments within the next 30 days to avoid foreclosing your home. In the event that you can’t repay your loan, the lender will be granted authority to sell your home through an auction. Once a property is sold, you should be ready for your eviction.

Foreclose Notices

For a foreclosure to get started, Indiana law requires that the right notices are sent to the parties involved.

  • Pre-foreclosure notice is sent to you by the foreclosing party, informing you of their plans to file a complaint in court regarding your mortgage delinquencies. This will give you 30 days to settle your dues, find financial assistance or avoid a foreclosure altogether.
  • Summons and complaints are provided by the foreclosing party, notifying you of the lawsuit filed. Within 20 days upon receipt, you must file a written response with the court.
  • Notice regarding a foreclosure settlement conference usually comes with the summons, if the home to be foreclosed is your primary residence. This gives you an opportunity to participate in a conference where you and your lender can work out alternatives to foreclosure.
  • Notice of sale is posted by the sheriff at the courthouse before a property is auctioned off at a foreclose sale. You will be given the same copy as well.
  • Notice to leave will be sent to you after your property is sold. As an extension of a foreclosure action, this notice gives lenders the right to evict you out of your home.

Summary

In Indiana, a foreclosure starts with you being served a notice of pre-foreclosure.

You will be given 30 days to repay your mortgage or avoid foreclosure, before a lawsuit is filed in court.
If you are able to pay before the court enters judgment, your loan will be reinstated and the foreclosure procedure stayed.

A notice of sale will be sent to you, informing you of the impending foreclose sale of your home.
Once a property is sold, you will receive a notice to leave, which basically translates to an eviction.

]]> Short Sale vs. Foreclosure


If you’re a homeowner, you’ve probably heard about the words “foreclosure” and “short sale” but have no idea what they mean. This isn’t really a problem if you have a relatively clean financial slate and are able to repay your mortgage and other debts on time. But, if you’re struggling to make your repayments on time and feel like you’re on the verge of a financial meltdown, it’s time to get more information about these terms and find out how they can affect you, your family and your entire life.

What are short sales and foreclosures?

These two terms might sound like they mean the same things, but they’re actually greatly different. In a short sale, you still own your home, but you’ll have to work with your lender and agree that you’ll sell the property at less than the amount of your outstanding mortgage balance. You also have to reach an agreement about what you’ll do with the unpaid balance (also known as the deficiency). Your lender can either be satisfied with the proceeds from your home sale and won’t ask for any additional payment, or he can demand that you still pay the deficiency.

A foreclosure, on the other hand, happens when you fail to meet your mortgage repayments and your lender decides to repossess your house and assumes ownership of it. He’ll then put it up for sale so he can recoup at least some of the money he had lent to you. He can also demand that you pay the deficiency, which means you won’t only be evicted from your property but will also have another debt to pay (while facing many other consequences).

How will these affect you?

Generally, short sales have fewer negative effects than foreclosures. Here’s a couple of lists to guide you:

The Effects of Short Sale:

  • It can impact your credit score by only 50 or 100 points and affect you for only one year to 18 months.
  • It will usually be reported to credit bureaus as “Paid in Full” and most likely won’t appear on your credit history.
  • It will allow you to get a loan to buy another home right away, as long as you’ve been able to make your mortgage payments on time and have no record of payments that were late for 60 days or more. You’ll also get reasonable interest rates in two years or less and can have access to mortgages backed by the Federal National Mortgage Association (FNMA) after 24 months.
  • Since it won’t appear on your credit history, it won’t have any adverse effects on your employment opportunities. This means you can continue with your job or look for a new one without having to worry about your financial records.
  • If the sale of your property goes smoothly and successfully, your house will most likely be sold near the market value and will let your lender recoup most of his losses. This makes him less likely to ask you to pay your deficiency (i.e., the difference between your mortgage balance and your home’s sale price). If he does ask you to pay it, the amount will be considerably lower and will be easier for you to repay.

The Effects of Foreclosure:

  • It can damage your credit score by 300 or even more points and can affect your record for three years or more.
  • It will appear on your credit history for more than 10 years and will be viewed by the public, leaving a more or less permanent mark on your valuable record.
  • It will make it harder for you to get a mortgage for a new house and apply for other types of loans. For one thing, lenders will ask you to declare in your application if you’ve had a foreclosed home within the past few year. If you do, they’ll most likely charge you a higher interest rate than normal, and some might even refuse to give you a loan. It will take around 12 months or six years before you can get affordable interest rates from mortgage providers, and at least five years before you can have access to mortgages backed by the FNMA.
  • In many cases, it can have an adverse effect on your career. If you’re currently employed, your managers might see your home’s foreclosure as a reason to terminate you (especially if your job requires handling company finances). If you’re looking for a job, you might fail to impress future employers, particularly those who’ll run a credit check and take a close look at your financial history.
  • Homes that are foreclosed and sold as a real-estate owned property often get lower prices. This means your lender will most likely pursue you to pay the deficiency and leave you with an additional debt to pay.

Which one should you choose?

As you can see from the lists above, short sales have a minimal effect on your credit worthiness, loan opportunities and personal reputation and almost zero impact on your current career and future employment. This is not the case with foreclosure, which can affect almost every aspect of your life. With these in mind, it’s easy to see that short sales are almost always the better choice between the two options.

What should you do before going with short sales and foreclosures?

Before making a decision, the best thing you should do is to meet with your lender and show you’re willing to negotiate. Doing this lets your lender know that you’re not running away from your obligations and you want to repay your debts as much as possible.

Ask your lender if you can opt for a short sale instead of having your home foreclosed. Many creditors will most likely agree to this so they won’t have to deal with the costs and complexities of conducting a foreclosure. But don’t be too complacent; instead, do everything you can to convince your lender that you have no other way of repaying your mortgage. Of course, show him that the amount you’ll earn from short selling your property is near the average market price. This way, he’ll see that he’ll be receiving a fair price from the sale and won’t be tempted to foreclose your house.

If your lender agrees to a short sale, ask if he’ll waive the deficiency so you won’t have anything left to pay; if he won’t agree to this, ask if you can at least come up with a plan that lets you repay the deficiency with small monthly payments. This way, you’ll eventually clear your debts while still having enough money to cover your daily needs and get back up on your feet.

Whether you opt for a short sale or a foreclosure, you need to prepare yourself and your family for the major changes that will surely take place in your life. Track your spending to see where you save; for instance, you can discontinue your cable TV service or reduce the number of times you eat out in restaurants. The money you save from these cost-cutting measures should be in your emergency funds, which you can use when you’ll move out of your property and look for another place to live. This way, you can survive during the first few months after your short sale or foreclosure, and you won’t have to rely on your credit cards or borrow money from friends and relatives to get by.

Isn’t there any other choice?

Of course, there’s another choice! If your lender refuses to cooperate with you, one of the best steps you can take is to sell your house. This way, you’ll have full control over the entire process, ensuring you’ll sell your home at market value and at least recoup some of your investment.

Since you have debts to pay, it’s not avoidable to go through the traditional route. This takes up a huge amount of time since you’ll need to advertise your property, deal with real estate agents and look for potential buyers. Instead, look for other ways that let you sell your house in a quick and efficient way.

One such thing you can do is to find a company that buys properties as-is for cash. You’ll also dispose of your home within the shortest possible amount of time, keeping short sales and foreclosures at bay and ensuring you can pay your debts on time.

What is a lease option?

This refers to an agreement where you give your tenant a choice to purchase your home at the end of a particular rental period, and for an agreed option purchase price. While the agreement is in effect, you cannot offer the property for sale to anyone else, which may seem like a bad idea, especially if you prefer to make money right away. Still, there are benefits to this kind of arrangement.

How does an Indianapolis lease option works?

For instance, you have a house or condo rented for $2,000 with a 30% rent credit. This enables the tenant-buyer to put up a forced savings of $600 a month that will accumulate to $7,200 in a year. For a 5-year lease-option agreement, a tenant-buyer will be able to build up $36,000 of rent credit, enough to cover the cost of a property purchase.

If your home is a fixer-upper, you can also offer a rent credit, provided that the tenant-buyer will renovate the property.

There are plenty of ways to strike up a deal with Indianapolis lease option that will prove beneficial to both the lessee (tenant-buyer) and lessor (landlord).

What are the elements of an Indianapolis lease option?

  • The buyer must first purchase the option. The fee is non-refundable, and usually costs 1% to 2% of the purchase price of the property. In the event that the tenant-buyer decides not to buy the property at the end of the terms agreed upon, the money remains with you, the seller, for the very reason that the option fee is not a deposit, but a purchase of an option.
  • The purchase price is agreed upon beforehand, although it can be decided when the option is exercised. In this case, the price will be based on the most current appraised value. It can also be equal to the price of a similar house within a neighborhood as of a date determined by an appraiser.
  • Set the amount of monthly rent payment that should be credited to the purchase price. This is usually negotiable, but is often 25% of the rent that should be paid.
